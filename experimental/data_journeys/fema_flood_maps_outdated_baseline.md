# FEMA Flood Maps and the Stationarity Assumption

The Federal Emergency Management Agency's Flood Insurance Rate Maps (FIRMs) serve as the regulatory backbone of the National Flood Insurance Program (NFIP). They are the basis for the [federal mandatory-purchase requirement](https://www.fema.gov/flood-maps/know-your-risk/realtor-lending-insurance), which generally applies when a structure sits inside a Special Flood Hazard Area (SFHA) and is secured by a federally regulated, insured, or backed loan; lenders may also require flood insurance outside SFHAs at their discretion. The binary the maps create — inside or outside an SFHA — translates directly into purchase requirements, local building rules, and many households' perceptions of their own risk. A growing body of climatological and hydrological evidence indicates that the maps systematically understate flood exposure in a changing climate.

The [First Street Foundation](https://www.prnewswire.com/news-releases/first-street-foundation-releases-new-data-disclosing-the-flood-risk-of-every-home-in-the-contiguous-us-301084757.html) estimates 14.6 million U.S. properties face substantial flood risk, compared with the roughly 8.7 million properties inside FEMA's SFHAs — a difference of approximately 70 percent, or nearly 6 million households. The discrepancy appears in recent disasters. During Hurricane Harvey in 2017, the [Houston Chronicle](https://www.sej.org/headlines/most-homes-damaged-harvey-were-outside-flood-plain-data-show) reported that roughly three-quarters of damaged homes were outside FEMA's 100-year floodplain, while [Houston Public Media](https://www.houstonpublicmedia.org/articles/news/flooding/2026/02/16/543472/harris-county-flood-map-fema/) reported that about 68 percent of homes that flooded in Harris County were outside the SFHA. For Hurricane Debby in 2024, a [First Street recreation](https://firststreet.org/research-library/first-street-recreation-of-hurricane-debby-finds-majority-of-damaged-homes-outside-of-fema-flood-zones) found that 78 percent of damaged properties were outside FEMA flood zones, with approximately [$9.7 billion in damages](https://thehill.com/policy/energy-environment/4825776-hurricane-debbie-flood-damage-uninsured/) falling in areas without a federal flood insurance purchase requirement.

## Core Technical Roots

### Stationarity and the Historical Baseline

FIRMs draw on historical precipitation and streamflow records and, in their conventional form, do not adequately reflect heavy rainfall trends or the best available climate science. FEMA Administrator Deanne Criswell [acknowledged in 2022](https://www.smithsonianmag.com/smart-news/federal-flood-maps-are-outdated-because-of-climate-change-fema-director-says-180980725/) that federal maps are "really focused on riverine flooding and coastal flooding" and often fail to capture record rainfall events driven by climate change. Analytically, this amounts to a working assumption of **stationarity** — that past patterns remain reliable predictors of future flood frequency — which is increasingly difficult to defend as extreme precipitation intensifies.

The Houston case illustrates how far the underlying climatology can drift from the maps in use. Until 2018, Texas flood mapping relied on [Technical Paper 40 (TP-40)](https://www.weather.gov/media/owp/oh/hdsc/docs/TechnicalPaper_No40.pdf), a rainfall frequency atlas authored by D.M. Hershfield and published by the U.S. Weather Bureau in 1961. When NOAA released [Atlas 14 Volume 11 for Texas in 2018](https://www.noaa.gov/media-release/noaa-updates-texas-rainfall-frequency-values) — one year after Harvey — the updated rainfall frequency estimates revised the Houston 100-year, 24-hour rainfall total from [12.5 inches to 17.9 inches, an increase of about 43 percent](https://climatediscovery.org/atlas-14-texas-the-100-year-storm-is-now-the-25-year-storm-already/). As [Community Impact reported](https://communityimpact.com/houston/city-county/2018/09/27/noaa-rainfall-data-houston/), events previously classified as 100-year storms became, under the updated statistics, substantially more frequent. In Houston-area 24-hour rainfall terms specifically, the new 100-year depth approaches what the older statistics classified as a 500-year depth: the regulatory categories remain intact, but the underlying rainfall climatology they were calibrated against has shifted.

Under [42 U.S.C. § 4101(e)](https://uscode.house.gov/view.xhtml?req=%28title%3A42+section%3A4101+edition%3Aprelim%29), FEMA is required, at least once every five years, to assess the need to revise and update floodplain areas and flood-risk zones — a duty to review, not a guarantee of new maps. In practice the study-to-adoption cycle for actual map updates has often taken significantly longer. A [2017 DHS Office of Inspector General audit](https://www.oig.dhs.gov/sites/default/files/assets/2017/OIG-17-110-Sep17.pdf) found significant weaknesses in FEMA's flood-mapping oversight and in the accuracy of a large share of effective maps.

### The Pluvial Flooding Gap

FIRMs primarily map **fluvial** (river) and **coastal** surge flooding. They do not systematically represent **pluvial** flooding — rainfall that overwhelms drainage systems independent of proximity to a river or coastline. As the [Washington Post reported in 2022](https://www.washingtonpost.com/climate-environment/interactive/2022/fema-flood-risk-maps-failures/), experts cited in its review of the program noted that FEMA flood maps largely do not attempt to model urban rainfall flooding, and FEMA officials have acknowledged that the maps mainly cover coastal and larger riverine watersheds.

Houston offers a quantitative illustration of the scale of land-cover change driving urban pluvial risk. Between 1997 and 2017, [the Rice Kinder Institute](https://kinder.rice.edu/urbanedge/rapid-urbanization-houston-how-it-happened-and-why-it-matters) reports that Houston's impervious surfaces — pavement and buildings — grew by about 1,000 square kilometers (approximately 386 square miles), an area larger than the combined land area of Boston, Philadelphia, Pittsburgh, and Washington D.C. Because pluvial risk falls outside FEMA's standard regulatory framework, properties exposed primarily to urban drainage failure are generally not flagged on FIRMs regardless of how often local rainfall exceeds drainage capacity.

### The Zone X False Negative

FEMA's binary classification — SFHA versus "Zone X" (minimal to moderate risk) — drives the federal mandatory-purchase requirement, since lenders do not require flood insurance for properties outside an SFHA. The result is a sharp difference in coverage between the two categories. According to a [2024 Congressional Budget Office report](https://www.cbo.gov/publication/60510), 18 percent of at-risk properties inside Special Flood Hazard Areas were covered by an NFIP policy, compared with only 4 percent of at-risk properties outside SFHAs; in inland communities the take-up rate among at-risk properties was likewise about 4 percent. First Street's analysis indicates that a majority of the properties it identifies as at substantial risk but outside FEMA's SFHAs are located in Zone X — precisely the category where the federal signal does not require coverage.

## Social Consequences and Equity

### The Insurance Gap in Practice

During Harvey, an [industry analyst study reported by ABC13 / KTRK](https://abc13.com/report-70-percent-of-flooded-properties-uninsured/2420749/) found that approximately 70 percent of flooded properties carried no flood insurance. Residents had generally made financial decisions consistent with the federal risk designations in effect at the time, since most of these properties sat outside SFHAs and faced no mandatory-purchase requirement.

The pattern is not unique to Harvey. Drawing on decades of NFIP claims data, research by Kevin Smiley summarized by the [Rice Kinder Institute](https://kinder.rice.edu/urbanedge/outdated-and-inaccurate-fema-flood-maps-fail-fully-capture-risk) found that in Greater Houston, 47 percent of claims made to FEMA in the three decades before Hurricane Harvey came from properties outside the 100-year flood zones. Claim patterns had indicated the gap between the regulatory designations and the distribution of actual flood losses well before Harvey.

### Racial and Geographic Inequity

The map gap is not evenly distributed across communities. Research by [Smiley and colleagues](https://theconversation.com/homes-are-flooding-outside-femas-100-year-flood-zones-and-racial-inequality-is-showing-through-146728) found that while mapped flood exposure was relatively even across racial groups in Houston, Black and Hispanic residents were disproportionately affected by flooding *outside* FEMA zones — the category where no insurance is mandated.

A 2022 study in *Nature Climate Change* by [Wing et al.](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41558-021-01265-6) found that projected increases in U.S. flood risk under climate change will disproportionately affect Black communities, particularly in the Gulf Coast region. A [2025 study in *Scientific Reports*](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-95120-9) by Flores, Collins, and Grineski identified a statistically significant association between the Black composition of U.S. metropolitan neighborhoods and exposure to federally overlooked pluvial flood risk, which the authors link to longstanding patterns of under-investment in drainage infrastructure.

### The Development Feedback Loop: Camp Mystic

The Camp Mystic case in Kerr County, Texas, shows how property owners can shape the maps that subsequently evaluate them. According to [reporting by the Associated Press via the Texas Tribune](https://www.texastribune.org/2025/07/12/camp-mystic-flood-plain-FEMA/) and [NPR](https://www.npr.org/2025/07/12/nx-s1-5465564/fema-removed-camp-mystic), FEMA placed Camp Mystic within a Special Flood Hazard Area in its 2011 Kerr County flood map. In response to appeals, FEMA amended the map in 2013 to remove 15 buildings at the original Camp Mystic Guadalupe site from the hazard area. Following further appeals, FEMA removed about 15 additional structures in 2019 and 2020 — these at the nearby Camp Mystic Cypress Lake sister site, which opened to campers in 2020 as part of the camp's expansion and which suffered less damage in the 2025 flood. On July 4, 2025, a flash flood on the Guadalupe River killed 27 people at Camp Mystic — predominantly young campers and two counselors. Camp director and co-owner [Dick Eastland](https://fortune.com/2025/07/07/richard-dick-eastland-camp-mystic-owner-director-death-texas-flooding/), who had run the camp with his wife since 1974, also died after attempting to lead a group of girls from their cabin to safety.

The camp's removed buildings were, by the regulatory record, outside the 100-year floodplain at the time of the flood. This illustrates a feedback pattern within an otherwise threshold-based system: regulated parties can petition to reshape the very designations used to determine whether they need to prepare for, insure against, or regulate future risk.

### Adaptation Lag and Risk Rating 2.0

The pace at which updated maps reach the regulatory record has been a recurring constraint. Harris County's [MAAPnext re-mapping effort](https://www.houstonchronicle.com/news/houston-texas/environment/article/fema-flood-maps-harris-county-2026-20305161.php) has been delayed multiple times, with officials now targeting an early-2026 draft release — nearly nine years after Harvey. During the intervening period, rebuilding and new development in Harris County has proceeded under regulations tied to the pre-Atlas 14 baseline.

In 2021, FEMA implemented [Risk Rating 2.0](https://www.fema.gov/flood-insurance/risk-rating), which prices NFIP premiums based on property-specific characteristics rather than broad zones. Risk Rating 2.0 changes the pricing of policies but does not change the FIRMs themselves. Because the mandatory-purchase requirement remains tied to the SFHA boundary, properties in Zone X continue to fall outside the insurance mandate even where property-specific modeling would assign them meaningful risk.

## Conclusion

The FIRM turns an uncertain, modeled estimate of flood hazard into a binary regulatory fact. Historical rainfall records, a riverine and coastal emphasis with limited treatment of pluvial flooding, slow update cycles, and the SFHA / Zone X cut each strip uncertainty from the result, so by the time it reaches a homeowner the designation simply determines whether insurance is required and how much risk they believe they carry. Across Harvey, Debby, Camp Mystic, and the broader pluvial gap, the cost of being wrong falls largely on the people the maps describe rather than on the institution producing them. Atlas 14, MAAPnext, and Risk Rating 2.0 narrow specific gaps but leave the underlying structure intact: a probabilistic estimate of a non-stationary hazard is still being used as a categorical trigger for legal and financial obligations.
