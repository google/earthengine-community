# Indonesia's Peat Map Conflict and the Governance of Fire Risk

Indonesia's forest, land, and peat fires have produced several major transboundary haze crises over the past three decades, including the 1997-1998, [2006](https://www.cifor.org/press-release/media-backgrounder-regions-fires-and-haze-problem-needs-clarity-on-causes-consequences-and-targeted-responses/), [2013](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371%2Fjournal.pone.0135118), 2015, and 2019 episodes; this essay focuses on the especially policy-shaping 1997-1998, 2015, and 2019 fire seasons and on the mapping conflicts that have complicated peat protection, restoration, and accountability. The 1997-1998 fires burned roughly 5 to 12 million hectares during an intense El Nino, depending on method and geography; estimates vary, and a [CIFOR review](https://www.cifor-icraf.org/knowledge/publication/32565/) cites figures at the upper end of that range. The 2015 fire season burned about [2.6 million hectares](https://openknowledge.worldbank.org/entities/publication/391e19f4-18f7-56c2-8c28-bb89cc9e7d6f) of forest and land and produced economic damages that the World Bank estimated at approximately US$16.1 billion, equivalent to 1.9 percent of Indonesia's 2015 GDP. A [Harvard-Columbia modeling study](http://doi.org/10.1088/1748-9326/11/9/094023) led by Shannon Koplitz and published in *Environmental Research Letters* estimated that PM2.5 from the 2015 smoke caused "upwards of 100,000" excess adult deaths across Indonesia, Malaysia, and Singapore, with mortality from exposure roughly doubling relative to the 2006 event (from about 38,000 to about 100,000); the Indonesian government [rejected that estimate](https://www.theguardian.com/environment/2016/sep/21/indonesia-dismisses-study-showing-forest-fire-haze-killed-more-than-100000-people) and attributed 24 deaths to the haze. According to [Global Forest Watch analysis by the World Resources Institute](https://www.wri.org/insights/intense-forest-fires-threaten-derail-indonesias-progress-reducing-deforestation), 40 percent of 2015 fire alerts and 42 percent of 2019 fire alerts in Indonesia were detected on peatland, even though peat covers a much smaller share of the national land area.

A recurring condition behind these fires is that Indonesia's land is represented through multiple, incompatible maps. [Forest Watch Indonesia reported in 2013](https://www.opengovpartnership.org/wp-content/uploads/2017/05/case-study_Indonesia_One-Map-Policy_0.pdf) that approximately 14.7 million hectares of land were subject to overlapping licenses issued by different ministries and subnational authorities, with roughly 7 million hectares of natural forest falling inside those overlaps. Peat depth -- the physical property that historically helped determine whether a parcel was protected under [Presidential Decree 32/1990](https://leap.unep.org/en/countries/id/national-legislation/presidential-decree-ri-no-32-1990-management-protected-areas) -- cannot be determined from satellite imagery alone; accurate peat-thickness maps require [field drilling and extensive borehole data](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7227361/), which the peer-reviewed literature describes as costly and labor-intensive. The institutional response to this layered problem has included the [One Map Policy](https://www.fig.net/resources/proceedings/fig_proceedings/fig2021/papers/ts08.2/TS08.2_abidin_lien_et_al_10887_abs.pdf), the [2019 move](https://news.mongabay.com/2019/07/dangerous-new-regulation-puts-indonesias-carbon-rich-peatlands-at-risk/) toward peat-dome-peak designation and water-table management, and the [Peatland and Mangrove Restoration Agency (BRGM)](https://news.mongabay.com/2021/01/indonesia-renews-peatland-mangrove-restoration-agency-brgm/), whose combined peat-and-mangrove task period ran from 2020 through December 31, 2024 under [Presidential Regulation 120/2020](https://www.peraturan.go.id/id/perpres-no-120-tahun-2020).

## Core Technical Roots

### Overlapping Concessions and the 14.7 Million Hectare Gap

In 2013, Forest Watch Indonesia reported that approximately [14.7 million hectares of land](https://www.opengovpartnership.org/wp-content/uploads/2017/05/case-study_Indonesia_One-Map-Policy_0.pdf) were subject to overlapping licenses -- issued by the Ministry of Forestry, the Ministry of Agriculture, the Ministry of Energy and Mineral Resources, and subnational authorities -- and that approximately 7 million hectares of natural forest lay within these conflicting license footprints. Where a parcel is simultaneously designated, for example, as a forestry concession and an oil-palm plantation under different ministries' maps, it becomes difficult to determine which agency's rules apply, which actor is liable for a fire, or whether a given clearing is legal.

Post-*Reformasi* decentralization shifted substantial permitting authority to subnational governments. [Law No. 22 of 1999](https://openknowledge.fao.org/server/api/core/bitstreams/1245c09c-c0f2-41b9-9bd4-3758f1dad736/content) devolved many land-affairs responsibilities to provincial and district governments; it was subsequently superseded by Law 32/2004 and then by [Law 23/2014](https://peraturan.bpk.go.id/Details/38685/uu-no-23-tahun-2014), which recentralized selected functions (notably forestry) at the provincial level while leaving plantation and location permitting largely with governors or district heads (*bupati*), who issue *izin lokasi* after review of existing rights over the land. In practice, documented [audits and investigations](https://news.mongabay.com/2022/05/indonesias-revocation-of-palm-oil-mining-permits-marred-by-maladministration/) have found that plantation and mining permits have been issued with administrative violations -- including missing location permits and inclusion of protected forest estate -- and that the revocation process itself has been affected by maladministration. Analysis published by [Forest Trends](https://www.forest-trends.org/wp-content/uploads/imported/indonesia-case_6-19-14-pdf.pdf) documents cases in which subnational permits issued in violation of forest classifications contributed to the broader overlap problem.

### The 3-Meter Threshold and Measurement Uncertainty

Indonesian peat protection was long organized around a depth threshold. As documented in a peer-reviewed review of Indonesian peatland policy in [*Soil Security*](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2667006222000466), [Presidential Decree No. 32 of 1990](https://leap.unep.org/en/countries/id/national-legislation/presidential-decree-ri-no-32-1990-management-protected-areas) on the management of protected areas was the "foremost regulation" concerning peat management at the national level and established that peat less than 3 meters in perpendicular depth could be used for agriculture and plantation purposes, while peat 3 meters or more was treated as protected. Some summaries specify the original criterion as peat 3 meters or more in upstream river and swamp areas, so the threshold should be read as a protected-area criterion rather than as a perfect parcel-by-parcel ecological boundary. This created a regulatory regime in which a single threshold value -- measured in the field -- could determine whether land was protected or available for conversion.

Peat depth cannot be determined from satellite imagery alone. As [peer-reviewed peat-mapping research](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7227361/) notes, accurate peat-thickness maps require field drilling and extensive borehole data, and creating high-accuracy maps is costly and labor-intensive. Because the 3-meter rule hinges on a field-measured value, the classification of any given parcel near the threshold depends on where and how densely boreholes are located -- a methodological sensitivity that is increasingly addressed in the literature through digital soil mapping and interpolation techniques aimed at [reducing the amount of field drilling required](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S001670611731306X) while maintaining accuracy.

### The 2019 Move Toward Peat-Dome Designation

In 2019, the Ministry of Environment and Forestry issued [Regulation P.10/2019](https://news.mongabay.com/2019/07/dangerous-new-regulation-puts-indonesias-carbon-rich-peatlands-at-risk/), moving implementation toward **Peat Hydrological Units (KHG)**, **peat dome peak** designation, and water-table management rather than relying only on the older depth-threshold logic. Under the Ministry's framing, protection is concentrated on the dome peak as the core of the hydrological system, with water-table management (the "eko-hidro" approach) applied across the broader KHG. Critics, including Mongabay's reporting cited below, read the same rule as permitting concession holders to operate on deep peat outside the dome peak provided prescribed water-table levels are maintained.

The regulation aligns peat protection more closely with hydrology -- peat degrades when drained, regardless of depth -- but critics argue that it reduces the practical reach of strict depth-based protection. As [reported by Mongabay](https://news.mongabay.com/2019/07/dangerous-new-regulation-puts-indonesias-carbon-rich-peatlands-at-risk/), the new rule restricts mandatory protection to peat domes, and deep-peat areas outside the dome peak "with protected status were opened for exploitation ... as long as the water table was properly managed." Irwansyah Reza Lubis of Wetlands International Indonesia warned in the same article that "if the new regulation is used by concession owners to keep operating, then it's going to be dangerous," arguing that peat protection "has to have a scope of an entire landscape." Analyst Anggalia Putri of Yayasan Madani Berkelanjutan flagged an internal contradiction noted in the article: a 2016 presidential regulation classifies drained protected peat as "damaged," which complicates any drainage-based management approach on land that would have been protected under the 3-meter rule.

### Map Inconsistency as an Evidentiary Problem

Map plurality has concrete legal consequences. In prosecutions of concession holders for fires on peatland, defense arguments have exploited inconsistencies between different ministry maps and between national and local maps: if the ministerial map, the district permit map, and the peat-distribution map disagree on where a boundary lies or whether a parcel is protected peat, the underlying factual basis for the charge can be contested. Summaries by the [NUS Asia-Pacific Centre for Environmental Law](https://law.nus.edu.sg/wp-content/uploads/2020/04/APCEL-Rep1801.pdf) and by [Mongabay](https://news.mongabay.com/2019/07/dangerous-new-regulation-puts-indonesias-carbon-rich-peatlands-at-risk/) describe cases in which cartographic disagreement has been one recurring evidentiary vulnerability in enforcement, alongside broader institutional and legal constraints.

## The One Map Policy

### Institutional Design

The One Map Policy (OMP) was formalized under [Presidential Regulation 9/2016](https://www.fig.net/resources/proceedings/fig_proceedings/fig2021/papers/ts08.2/TS08.2_abidin_lien_et_al_10887_abs.pdf), which set a 1:50,000-scale target for a unified national map and directed ministries to synchronize their thematic layers on a shared geospatial reference. As originally designed in 2016, the program covered 85 thematic maps drawn from 19 ministries and agencies across 34 provinces, grouped into categories including infrastructure, forestry, natural resources and disaster, regional boundaries, licenses and permits, regional planning, and special areas. By late 2015, the head of BIG had [stipulated 41 custodians](https://www.opengovpartnership.org/wp-content/uploads/2017/05/case-study_Indonesia_One-Map-Policy_0.pdf) of thematic geospatial information, assigning specific agencies responsibility for specific data layers, and the [One Map geoportal](https://publicpolicyindonesia.wordpress.com/2018/03/04/mapping-out-the-progress-of-indonesias-one-map-policy-initiative/) was publicly launched in December 2018, with access to sensitive layers restricted to government users. The scope has since expanded: as of July 2024, [Geoportal One Map 2.0 reporting](https://bizindo.com/indonesia-geoportal-one-map-2-0/) and the [national satupeta.go.id portal](https://satupeta.go.id/) indicate the program had compiled 151 thematic maps from roughly 22-23 central ministries and agencies across all 38 Indonesian provinces, and Geoportal One Map 2.0 was launched on July 18, 2024.

### What the OMP Resolves and What It Does Not

The OMP harmonizes state-issued layers onto a common base map. It does not automatically include maps produced outside the state -- in particular, participatory maps of customary (*wilayah adat*) territories produced by indigenous communities and NGOs. As a [2020 Jakarta Post article](https://www.thejakartapost.com/news/2020/09/04/concerns-of-transparency-inclusivity-raised-as-one-map-nears-completion.html) on the OMP's near-completion noted, civil-society advocates have warned that unifying state layers without integrating customary-land data risks entrenching concession boundaries in the authoritative record while leaving customary claims outside it. Scholarly analysis by [Astuti et al.](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0962629822000658) argues, in the authors' reading, that One Map programs in Indonesia and Myanmar function as instruments of state territorialization as much as dispute-resolution tools: in their framing, the layers that enter the map are the claims that become visible to the state, and those that do not, recede.

## Institutional Response: From BRG to BRGM

### The Restoration Target

In January 2016, following the 2015 crisis, President Joko Widodo established the **Peatland Restoration Agency (BRG)**. Early official reporting described the new agency as addressing around [2 million hectares](https://setkab.go.id/en/president-jokowi-establishes-peat-land-restoration-agency-brg/) of peatlands, while later accounts of BRG/BRGM planning and implementation often used larger operational restoration targets of roughly 2.4-2.6 million hectares across seven priority provinces -- Riau, Jambi, South Sumatra, West Kalimantan, Central Kalimantan, South Kalimantan, and Papua. The program used a "3R" strategy of rewetting (canal blocking and infrastructure), revegetation, and revitalization of community livelihoods. Later reporting by [Mongabay](https://news.mongabay.com/2021/01/indonesia-renews-peatland-mangrove-restoration-agency-brgm/) described the operational target as roughly 1.7 million hectares inside active concessions and 900,000 hectares outside.

### Asymmetric Progress Inside and Outside Concessions

By the end of 2020, according to [Mongabay](https://news.mongabay.com/2021/01/indonesia-renews-peatland-mangrove-restoration-agency-brgm/), BRG had restored 835,288 hectares of peatland outside concessions -- 94 percent of its 900,000-hectare non-concession target. Restoration inside concessions was handled separately: plantation and pulpwood companies whose concessions overlap peat landscapes conducted parallel restoration efforts monitored by the environment ministry. The institutional split -- BRG/BRGM acting directly on non-concession land while the Ministry of Environment supervised company-led restoration inside concessions -- meant the agency's direct, verifiable on-the-ground reach was concentrated where the state controlled the land directly. That phrasing is narrower than saying all restoration outside concessions outpaced concession restoration: the environment ministry has separately claimed substantial company-led restoration in concession areas, including [3.9 million hectares in concession areas and 5.5 million hectares overall](https://en.antaranews.com/news/316380/indonesia-has-restored-55-million-hectares-of-peatlands-ministry).

### Expansion to BRGM and the 2024 Wind-Down

In late 2020, the agency's mandate was renewed and expanded and it was rebranded as the **Peatland and Mangrove Restoration Agency (BRGM)**. Peatland work continued across the original seven peat provinces (Riau, Jambi, South Sumatra, Central Kalimantan, South Kalimantan, West Kalimantan, and Papua), while the mangrove mandate covered nine provinces -- North Sumatra, Riau, Bangka-Belitung, the Riau Islands, West Kalimantan, East Kalimantan, North Kalimantan, Papua, and West Papua -- so that the combined geographic footprint spanned roughly thirteen provinces in total rather than the peat program itself expanding to thirteen. As described in the [Mongabay 2021 article](https://news.mongabay.com/2021/01/indonesia-renews-peatland-mangrove-restoration-agency-brgm/), a deputy environment minister characterized the BRGM mandate as a combined peat-and-mangrove restoration target of "maybe around 2 million hectares" -- roughly 1.2 million hectares of leftover peat from the earlier BRG period plus approximately 600,000 hectares of new mangrove rehabilitation.

BRGM's task period ended on December 31, 2024 under [Presidential Regulation 120/2020](https://www.peraturan.go.id/id/perpres-no-120-tahun-2020). According to [tanahair.net reporting](https://en.tanahair.net/2024/12/30/the-end-of-an-era-indonesias-peatland-and-mangrove-restoration-agency-to-be-dissolved/) and subsequent [ANTARA reporting](https://en.antaranews.com/news/349269/indonesias-brgm-restores-16m-hectares-of-peatland-84k-mangroves), the agency recorded restoration of approximately 1.6 million hectares of peatland and rehabilitation of about 84,000 hectares of mangrove over its tenure, and entered a liquidation phase with assets and responsibilities transferring to relevant ministries. A Ministry of Environment peatland-and-mangrove management center, BPEGM, was established by [Ministerial Regulation No. 8/2025](https://pasal.id/peraturan/permen/permenklhbph-no-08-tahun-2025) as one successor implementation and coordination unit, but post-BRGM responsibilities appear divided across multiple ministries rather than transferred to a single institutional replacement. The record therefore closes with BRG/BRGM's direct restoration activity concentrated outside concessions, while concession-area restoration remained largely company-led and ministry-supervised.

## Social Consequences and Equity

### Uneven Distribution of Haze Exposure

Haze exposure from forest, land, and peat fires is concentrated in communities downwind of the main fire provinces -- Riau, Jambi, South Sumatra, and Central Kalimantan -- and the heaviest health burden falls on residents with the fewest resources to relocate, purchase filtration, or take time off work. The gap between the Koplitz et al. modeled burden and the 24 death figure reported by the Indonesian government reflects differences in method: modeled attributable deaths from population-level PM2.5 exposure versus deaths certified to haze as a direct, acute cause.

### Indigenous and Customary Lands

Communities whose claims rest on customary rather than concession-based tenure have been among the most affected by the dual mapping problem. Where customary territories overlap with concessions in the state record, state-recognized concessions often carry greater practical weight in enforcement and permitting than customary claims not incorporated into official map layers, even when local residents possess long-standing use rights. [Human Rights Watch's 2021 report *Why Our Land?*](https://www.hrw.org/report/2021/06/03/why-our-land/oil-palm-expansion-indonesia-risks-peatlands-and-livelihoods) documents cases in which oil-palm expansion on peat proceeded over the objections of indigenous communities whose customary maps were not incorporated into the authoritative layers. The OMP's current architecture -- which consolidates state layers but does not require integration of participatory maps -- does not resolve this asymmetry.

### Corporate Accountability and the Enforcement Record

Prosecutions for peatland fires have produced mixed outcomes. Civil suits have yielded several large judgments against plantation companies, but criminal convictions have been less frequent and collection of damages has often lagged. The cartographic foundation matters here, but it is not the only constraint: when the government cannot produce a single authoritative map of peat depth, concession boundaries, and burned area, defendants can contest the factual predicate of a charge; proof burdens, corporate-liability doctrine, enforcement capacity, political economy, and judicial discretion also shape outcomes. As the OMP matures and as Regulation P.10/2019's peat-dome and water-table framework is implemented, the clarity of the evidentiary record could improve -- but only to the extent that dome delineations and water-table monitoring are themselves treated as authoritative rather than contested.

## Conclusion

The peat map conflict is not only a technical problem of data harmonization; it is a question of which representation of the land is treated as authoritative when representations disagree. Indonesia's institutional response -- the One Map Policy, BRG/BRGM, and the 2019 move toward peat-dome-peak designation and water-table management -- has narrowed specific gaps without resolving the underlying structure. Civil-society critics argue that state layers are being harmonized faster than customary and participatory layers are incorporated; BRG/BRGM's direct, verifiable restoration work was concentrated outside concessions even as the environment ministry has reported substantial company-led restoration inside concessions; and the regulatory definition of protected peat has moved toward a hydrological standard whose field implementation is itself contested. [Global Forest Watch](https://www.wri.org/insights/intense-forest-fires-threaten-derail-indonesias-progress-reducing-deforestation) and subsequent reporting indicate that peak burned area in the years since 2015 has not returned to 2015 levels, though 2019 and 2023 El Nino seasons produced substantial fires; the risk has persisted, and the underlying condition -- that multiple, imperfectly aligned maps are used to make categorical determinations about liability, protection, and restoration -- remains in place.
